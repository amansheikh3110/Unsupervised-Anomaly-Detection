--- SLIDE: Introduction ---
• Problem we are solving:
  – In manufacturing, visual inspection for defects is critical but costly and
    error-prone when done manually.
  – Supervised methods need large labelled defect data, which is scarce in
    industry; defects are rare, diverse, and often unseen during training.
  – Existing unsupervised methods (e.g. reconstruction-based) detect anomalies
    via reconstruction error but often miss high-level semantic defects
    (structural anomalies, missing components).

• High-level idea of our solution:
  – We build an unsupervised system that learns only from normal (good) images.
  – Phase 1 (current): Baseline using a convolutional autoencoder; anomaly
    score = reconstruction error (MSE).
  – Phase 2 (planned): Replace pixel-level reconstruction with I-JEPA
    (Image-based Joint-Embedding Predictive Architecture), which learns
    semantic representations in latent space and predicts masked regions in
    that space. Anomalies are then detected as deviations from the learned
    normal distribution in the latent space.

2. MOTIVATION
================================================================================

--- SLIDE: Motivation ---
• Why this project is necessary:
  – Manual inspection: slow, expensive, inconsistent; not scalable.
  – Supervised defect detection: requires many labelled defect samples; poor
    generalization to novel defect types (Ruff et al., 2021; Bergmann et al., 2019).
  – Reconstruction-based unsupervised methods (e.g. autoencoders): optimize
    pixel-level reconstruction, which does not guarantee semantic
    consistency; they can fail on structural or semantic anomalies and are
    sensitive to texture (Baur et al.; you may cite your "Unreliability of
    autoencoders" paper here for statistical evidence).

• Research gap:
  – Need for methods that learn semantic, invariant representations of
    "normal" and detect anomalies as deviations in representation space,
    without any defect labels.

• Real-world impact:
  – Automated visual inspection, quality control in manufacturing, surface
    defect detection, semiconductor and packaging verification (Bergmann
    et al., 2019; synopsis).

--- SLIDE: Literature Review — Traditional Autoencoder ---
• Source: Use your "Traditional Autoencoder" paper from the papers folder.

• Core idea:
  – Autoencoder: encoder maps input x to latent z; decoder maps z to
    reconstruction x̂. Trained to minimize reconstruction loss (e.g. MSE).
  – In anomaly detection: train only on normal data; at test time, high
    reconstruction error is used as anomaly score (e.g. Sakurada & Yairi, 2014;
    or reference from your PDF).

• Key equation (include on slide):
  – Reconstruction loss (MSE):  L_rec = (1/n) Σ ‖x − x̂‖²
  – Anomaly score:  s(x) = ‖x − Dec(Enc(x))‖²  (or mean over pixels).

• Advantages:
  – Unsupervised; no defect labels needed.
  – Simple to implement and train; interpretable (reconstruction error).

• Limitations:
  – Optimizes pixel-level reconstruction, not semantic fidelity; can
    reconstruct some anomalies well (low error) and some normals poorly
    (high error).
  – May focus on low-level texture; weak on structural/semantic anomalies.

• Diagram to use:
  – On this slide, use the figure from your traditional_autoencoder.pdf that
    shows the standard bottleneck architecture (encoder → latent → decoder).
  – Instruction: Open "traditional_autoencoder.pdf", find the figure

--- SLIDE: Literature Review — I-JEPA ---
• Source: Assran et al. (2023), "Self-Supervised Learning from Images with a
  Joint-Embedding Predictive Architecture" (I-JEPA). Use the I-JEPA paper
  from your papers folder.

• Core idea:
  – I-JEPA predicts representations of target image regions from context
    regions in the same image, in a joint embedding space. It does not
    reconstruct pixels; it predicts latent representations of masked patches
    from visible (context) patches.
  – Components: context encoder (processes visible patches), target encoder
    (processes masked patches; often EMA-updated), predictor (maps context
    representation to predicted target representation).
  – Training: minimize distance (e.g. MSE) between predicted and target
    representations (with stop-gradient on target).

• Key equation (from I-JEPA; include on slide):
  – Let s be context (visible) patches and t be target (masked) patches.
  – Context encoding:  z_s = f_θ(s);  target encoding:  z_t = g_ξ(t)  (ξ may
    be EMA of θ).
  – Prediction:  p_θ(z_s) ≈ z_t.
  – Loss (e.g. L2):  L = ‖p_θ(z_s) − stop_grad(z_t)‖²
  – Reference: Assran et al. (2023), Section 3; use the exact equation from
    the paper and cite it.

• Advantages:
  – Learns semantic, high-level representations; not tied to pixel
    reconstruction.
  – Predicts in latent space; robust to irrelevant pixel variations.
  – Well-suited for downstream tasks (e.g. anomaly detection as deviation
    in latent space).

• Limitations:
  – More complex than autoencoders; requires careful design of masking and
    architecture (Assran et al., 2023).

• Diagram to use:
  – Use the main method figure from the I-JEPA paper (typically shows
    context encoder, target encoder, predictor, and masking). Open
    "I-JEPA.pdf", find the figure that illustrates the architecture (often
    Figure 1 or 2). Insert it and cite: "Figure X from Assran et al. (2023)."

• Inline citation:
  – "I-JEPA (Assran et al., 2023) learns by predicting latent representations
    of masked image regions from context, avoiding pixel reconstruction and
    capturing semantic structure."

4. PROPOSED METHODOLOGY
================================================================================

--- SLIDE: Proposed Methodology — Overview ---
• Step-by-step solution:
  1. Data: Use MVTec AD (Bergmann et al., 2019). Training: only normal
     (good) images per object category. Test: normal + defective images.
  2. Preprocessing: Resize to 224×224; normalize with ImageNet mean/std
     (0.485, 0.456, 0.406 / 0.229, 0.224, 0.225).
  3. Baseline (current): Train convolutional autoencoder on normal images
     only. Anomaly score = MSE(input, reconstruction). Threshold from
     validation (e.g. best F1).
  4. Future (I-JEPA): Train I-JEPA on normal images (patchify → mask →
     context/target encoders → predictor → MSE in latent space). Extract
     context encoder embeddings for all normal images; fit normality model
     (e.g. Mahalanobis, k-NN, or One-Class SVM). At test: embedding →
     distance to normal model = anomaly score.
  5. Evaluation: ROC-AUC, average precision, F1, precision/recall (labels
     used only for evaluation).

• Create a single flow diagram with these components and arrows:

  [Data] MVTec AD (train: good only; test: good + defect)

  [Preprocessing] Resize 224×224, ImageNet normalize

  [Model]
  – Current: Autoencoder (Encoder → Latent z → Decoder → Reconstruction x̂).
  – Future: I-JEPA (Image → Patchify → Mask → Context Encoder → z_ctx;
    Target Encoder (EMA) → z_tgt; Predictor: z_ctx → p(z_tgt); Loss: ‖p − z_tgt‖²).

  Optimizer: Adam/AdamW. Save checkpoint.

  – I-JEPA: Embed image → score = distance to normal model (e.g. Mahalanobis).
      ↓
  [Evaluation] Compare scores to ground truth; compute ROC-AUC, AP, F1.
  (Optional: per-patch scores → heatmap for localization.)
